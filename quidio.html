The Problem

The Asking Experience

You have a question: for a project, an assignment, or maybe for work.
If you code, you might look to StackOverflow or GitHub. If you're a hobbyist, you might ask on Quora or a forum. With high hopes, you write out the question, hoping that a friendly stranger will help...
Most of the time, however, your question will slip through the cracks.
With so many questions, community volunteers will never get through everything.

The Answering Experience

You're an expert! Or maybe still a learner.
Either way, you have something to share with the world. This isn't a full-time gig, so you go to a Q&A site, or maybe a forum. But there are so many questions.
You can't seem to find the right ones, or the motivation to tackle the unending stream of requests.

The Solution: Quidio.

Quidio empowers both sides of this equation.
As an asker, you take agency in getting an answer with a bounty. As an answerer, you get compensated for your contribution, directly helping someone in the process.

The Name

Quid pro quo

Latin phrase

Quid pro quo is a Latin phrase used in English to mean an exchange of goods or services, in which one transfer is contingent upon the other; "a favor for a favor".

Quidio comes from the first word of the phrase "quid pro quo." Quidio is built on giving the asker agency in the attention their question receives and compensating the answerer for their value.

The co-founder experience

It's been great having two awesome co-founders to work on this with. It's really hard to do a startup alone, so these relationships are invaluable! Not to mention I have new life-long friends.

A timeless balancing act

Being a full-time student at Harvard is hard as it is. Balancing a startup on top of that is even harder. I really had to master time management in order to effectively pursue both my academic, startup, and other interests.

Starting a startup

There's a lot that goes into "starting a startup." I learned about the legal documentation necessary to have a real startup. Furthermore, I read dozens of books that taught me how to adopt a problem-centric approach to business. These skills are translateable beyond the startup world and will be useful in all my future ventures.
